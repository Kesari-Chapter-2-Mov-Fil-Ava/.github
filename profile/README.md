## Who Was C Sankaran Nair?

**Sir Chettur Sankaran Nair** was a towering figure in India's legal and political history. Born in 1857 in the Malabar region of British India (now in Kerala), he rose to become one of the earliest Indian judges in the Madras High Court and the President of the Indian National Congress in 1897.

A champion of civil rights and constitutional reform, Nair was deeply disturbed by the atrocities committed under British rule, most notably the **1919 Jallianwala Bagh massacre** in Amritsar. His resignation from the Viceroy’s Executive Council in protest marked one of the first major political stands by a high-ranking Indian official against the British regime.

## Film Synopsis

The movie dramatizes Nair’s battle against the British establishment following the Jallianwala Bagh massacre—a massacre that left over 400 Indian civilians dead when General Dyer ordered open fire on a peaceful gathering. This brutal act ignited national outrage, but the British tried to downplay it.

The film follows Nair's journey as he files a **defamation lawsuit** against Michael O’Dwyer, the then-Lieutenant Governor of Punjab who endorsed the massacre. His legal fight in a British court was an act of defiance, exposing the empire's brutality on an international stage. Through courtroom confrontations and personal struggles, the film paints a picture of a man who used intellect and law as his weapons against oppression.

## Historical Importance: Why This Story Matters

The **Jallianwala Bagh massacre** has long been recognized as a turning point in India’s struggle for independence. However, the judicial and moral response to it has received relatively little attention in popular media. By focusing on C Sankaran Nair’s resistance through lawful means, the film highlights a unique narrative: the use of **British legal systems** to challenge colonial injustice.

While many fought for freedom through protests and revolution, Nair chose a different path—by holding the empire accountable **in its own courtrooms**. His legal stand was not just symbolic, but transformative.
